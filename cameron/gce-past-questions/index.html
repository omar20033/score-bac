How to actually use past papers

01

Time yourself for real

Sit the full paper under exam time, not "as long as it takes." Timing pressure is part of what past papers are meant to train.

02

Mark it honestly

Compare against a marking guide where you can find one, and be strict with yourself — generous self-marking hides the gaps you actually need to close.

03

Track the pattern, not just the answer

After 4-5 papers in a subject, you'll notice which topics repeat. That pattern is more useful than any single paper's answers.

04

Fix the gap, then retest

If a topic keeps costing marks across multiple years' papers, stop and re-study that topic specifically before doing more papers.

Frequently asked questions

How many years of GCE past questions should I practice?
Aim for at least 5 years per subject if you can find them. Format and command words tend to stay stable for long stretches, so even 8-10 year old papers are useful for technique — just confirm against your current syllabus that the paper structure hasn't changed since.
Where can I find GCE O Level and A Level past questions?
Several community-run sites and apps host archives by subject — Kamerpower, GCE Panel, CameroonGCErevision (the Kawlo app), GCE Answers, and eStudyUniverse among them. Coverage and answer quality varies by subject and year, so it's worth checking more than one source for a given paper.
Do past questions come with answers or marking schemes?
Sometimes — but official Board marking schemes aren't widely published, so most "answers" online are proposed solutions from tutors or fellow students, not the Board's own scheme. Use them as a guide and verify anything that looks doubtful.
Is using past questions enough to pass GCE?
No — past papers train format, timing, and command-word recognition, but they don't replace actually learning the syllabus content. Pair topic revision with timed practice rather than relying on memorised answers.
What's "kawlo" in relation to GCE past questions?
Kawlo is a mobile app and study platform (run by the team behind CameroonGCErevision.com) that hosts a community-shared archive of GCE past questions. It's one of several third-party resources students use — not an official GCE Board product.
